# What would it actually take?

Not to fix everything. Just to stop optimizing against each other.

There's a version of this question that goes straight to policy — universal basic income, housing reform, zoning laws. That version is important and I'm not going to write it. Partly because other people already have, and partly because the interesting part isn't the mechanism. The interesting part is what happens to people when the floor is solid enough.

## The garden and the lawn

You know the difference between a garden you tend and a lawn that gets maintained. One you're in a relationship with. The other is a service. The garden has weeds you recognize. Tomatoes that came in weird this year. A corner where you tried something and it didn't work and you left the evidence because pulling it out felt dishonest. The lawn is a surface. It gets cut. It stays green. Nobody has a relationship with their lawn.

Houses work like this now. The house you live in is also the house you might sell. So you're making decisions about your own living space with one eye on the market — will this kitchen reno increase resale value, will the neighborhood "trend up," does the school district look good on paper. You're slightly absent from your own life. Not because you're shallow. Because the house is simultaneously a home and an investment, and investment colonizes the present. It's hard to fully inhabit a place you're also evaluating.

The mcmansion is the purest expression of this. Big lot, three-car garage facing the street, no front porch or a decorative one nobody sits on. Maximum interior square footage, minimum reason to be outside. The neighbors are right there — thirty feet away — and you might go years without learning their names. The house accidentally solved away the neighbor. Not on purpose. Space as status signal just turns out to be incompatible with space as lived-in place.

## Mixed-use

There's an old idea in urban planning that keeps getting rediscovered because it keeps being right. Mixed-use neighborhoods. The bakery on the ground floor, apartments above it. The school three blocks from the park that's two blocks from the grocery store. Overlapping paths.

The bakery is viable because enough people walk past it. Not because someone decided "we need a bakery here for community reasons." Just — the density is right, the zoning allows it, and the baker can afford the rent because the foot traffic exists. The relationship between you and the baker forms in the gaps between errands. You walk past on the way to somewhere else. You stop because it smells good. You come back because last time you stopped, the person behind the counter remembered your name. None of this was engineered. The conditions allowed it and the human thing happened on its own.

[Where does connection come from?](/prose/where-does-connection-come-from) talks about this — shared space, shared time, low stakes. The conditions are the work. The connection is the byproduct. Mixed-use is the spatial version of the same insight: you don't create relationships by pointing people at each other. You create them by giving people reasons to be in the same place, over and over, for reasons that have nothing to do with each other.

Suburban zoning did the opposite. Residential here, commercial there, schools somewhere else. Every errand is a car trip. You leave the house, enter the car, enter the destination, reverse the process. The space between home and everywhere else is windshield. Nobody forms a relationship through a windshield.

## The floor

Here's where it gets tricky, because I'm going to talk about what happens when people aren't scared, and I don't fully know.

But I can see what happens when they are. You start a college fund before the kid can talk. Not because you're obsessive — because if you don't, the math doesn't work. Enrichment activities become resume items by middle school. The house in the right school district costs more, so the mortgage is bigger, so both parents work more, so the kid is in after-school care, which needs to be enriching, which costs money. The optimization is completely rational given the constraints. Every individual decision makes sense. The aggregate is a life that's being planned instead of lived.

And the thing about a floor — a real one, solid, not going anywhere — is that it doesn't make people better. It makes the optimization unnecessary. The kid doesn't need a resume at twelve if the floor catches them at twenty-two. The house doesn't need to be an investment if losing it doesn't mean losing everything. You can tend the garden instead of maintaining the lawn because the garden's output isn't load-bearing anymore. It's just a garden.

The barrier below the floor is interesting because you don't experience it as a barrier. It's like electricity. Nobody wakes up and thinks "today I'm going to overcome the challenge of generating power." It's handled before you arrive. A solid floor works the same way. You don't feel brave for not optimizing your kid's childhood. You just don't do it, because the reason to do it isn't there, the same way you don't hand-crank a generator because the outlet works.

I don't know what people do with that freed-up attention. That's the honest answer. I can see what they stop doing — the defensive optimization, the planning-as-anxiety, the slight absence from their own present. What fills the space, I'm not sure. Maybe it's [the kind of connection](/prose/where-does-connection-come-from) that only happens when nobody's performing. Maybe it's something I can't predict from inside a world that hasn't built the floor yet.

## r/place

Reddit did this thing in 2017. A blank canvas. Every user could place one pixel every five minutes. That's it. No goals, no rewards, no content algorithm.

It was the most alive thing the internet did that decade. People coordinated to draw flags, logos, art, jokes. They defended territory. They negotiated borders between factions. They built things that required hundreds of people placing pixels for hours. The canvas was writable and shared and had no rules, and what emerged was — I don't know exactly what to call it. Collective authorship. A commons.

They ran it again in 2022. And 2023. Each time it was treated as an event, a gimmick, a curiosity. Something fun that Reddit does sometimes. Never as infrastructure. Never as a permanent feature of the platform. Because a writable shared canvas can't be monetized as engagement. There's nothing to scroll. Nobody's attention is being directed. The value is in the participation, and participation doesn't generate ad impressions.

There's a version of the internet where every surface is writable. Where the thing you're looking at is the thing you can change. `contenteditable` shipped in IE 5.5 — the browser has known how to do this since 2000. HTML5 standardized it. And then we built platforms on top of the web instead of making the web itself the platform. The barrier to having a voice didn't collapse when tools got easier. It would collapse when the surface you're already looking at is the surface you write on. We had that. We built walls around it instead.

[Why did things stop being personal?](/prose/why-did-things-stop-being-personal) follows the same arc — the personal web didn't die because the technology disappeared. The context changed. The assumption changed. The permission got buried under platforms that were good enough that the thing they couldn't do stopped seeming important.

## Cyberpunk got it backwards

Cyberpunk is intimate. Not despite the hostile world — because of it. When the threat is the ground and not the boundary, wall-watching stops being useful. You stop scanning for who's inside and who's outside. The person across the table from you isn't a potential competitor for the next rung up. They're just a person, sitting there, in the same rain.

The genre figured out something that utopian fiction usually misses. Utopia zooms out. It wants to show you the system — the gleaming city, the solved infrastructure, the policy that fixed everything. And in zooming out it loses the person. The individual life becomes a proof of concept for the system. "Look, she's happy — the system works." That's not a story. That's a brochure.

The intimacy of cyberpunk comes from the zoom staying close. One person's life. Their noodle shop. Their apartment. Their one friend who might betray them. You know what their morning looks like. You know what they can't afford. And because you're that close, the connection — when it happens — isn't an illustration of anything. It's just two people in a room, which is [all connection ever was](/prose/where-does-connection-come-from#the-bar-stool).

What would it take to write a utopia that stays that close? I keep circling this. Not a world where the problems are solved and everyone's fine — a world where the floor exists and one person's Tuesday is different because of it. The baker who doesn't check the zoning variance because the zoning was never wrong. The parent who doesn't start the college fund because the floor is there. The garden that's just a garden. Nothing dramatic. Just a life that's being lived instead of optimized.

I don't know how to write that yet. It might not be possible to make it interesting — a solved problem isn't a story. But I think the attempt matters, because right now the only futures that feel real in fiction are the broken ones, and that's doing something to what people believe is possible.
