# Why are you lonely?

Not alone. Lonely. You have people around you. Friends, family, coworkers, online communities. You talk to them. They talk to you. So why does it feel like nobody actually knows you?

## Nobody goes deep

Think about the last ten conversations. How many went past the surface?

Not "deep" as in heavy topics. Deep as in: the other person saw something about you that you didn't show on purpose. You learned something about them that surprised you. You left feeling *known*, not just heard.

Most conversations are exchanges. Information, opinions, jokes, updates. You talk about work, the weather, shared interests. It's pleasant. Might even be fun. But when it's over, has anything transferred? We know things *about* people — their job, their hobbies, their opinions. They know things about us. And it feels like enough. It feels like connection. You can go years before you notice it isn't.

There's this thing where you can spend an entire evening with someone and afterward neither of you could say what the other person was actually feeling. Not because you didn't ask. Because asking "how are you" is a script, and both of you know it's a script, and breaking the script is — what, exactly? Rude? Weird? It's not even that. It's that you'd have to answer honestly and you're not sure you want to.

And it's not that people are shallow. Most normal social contexts work against depth. You have ten minutes at the coffee machine. An hour at dinner. Depth needs open-ended time, and nobody schedules open-ended time. Groups make it worse — vulnerability in front of multiple people is risky, so groups default to surface-level and then everyone concludes that surface is all there is. You get a role. "The funny one," "the quiet one." People interact with the role. The role is comfortable. Breaking out of it disorients everyone, including you.

And underneath all of it, the coordination problem: if you go deep and the other person doesn't match, it's awkward. So nobody goes first. None of these are anyone's fault. They're just the default settings of human interaction. And defaults are powerful — most people never change them.

## Safe isn't the same as known

Maybe you have safe people. People who wouldn't judge you. People who'd listen if you really opened up.

But you still don't.

And that's — look, this is the part that actually hurts. Not in a poetic way. In the way where you're sitting across from someone who loves you and you can feel the distance and you can't figure out how to close it and you start wondering if something is wrong with you specifically. Because they're right there. Being kind. Being patient. And you still can't cross. It's not that they failed you. It's not that you failed them. It's that safety got you to the edge and you still couldn't jump, and you don't fully know why, and neither do they.

You can be completely yourself around someone and they still don't *see* you. Not because they're bad people — because seeing someone takes sustained attention, real curiosity, and the kind of conversation that most relationships never arrive at. Even the safe ones.

Safe people still default to comfortable patterns. You talk about the same topics. You fill the same roles. You enjoy each other's company. And it's good. But "good" and "known" are different things.

Then there's the [90% problem](/prose/whats-actually-wrong). Your friends are safe. Most of your family is safe. But "most" isn't "all." One unsafe person in a system can make the whole system feel unsafe. You can't be fully yourself at the dinner table if one person at the table makes it dangerous. So you perform a version of yourself that's safe for everyone, and nobody at the table — including the safe ones — ever meets the real you.

## The gap that doesn't get talked about

This might be the harder question. Even if everyone around you was perfectly safe, perfectly attentive, perfectly curious — could they know you?

Do *you* know you?

A lot of what ["what are labels anyway?"](/prose/what-are-labels-anyway) describes is people not seeing themselves clearly. Living inside old stories. Mistaking patterns for identity. How can someone else see you if you're still [figuring out what's there](/prose/who-am-i)?

And knowing yourself isn't just introspection. It's [doing things](/prose/how-do-i-do-things) and watching what they reveal — making something and noticing what came out, going somewhere unfamiliar and seeing what it stirred up. You can't fully share what you haven't fully found.

So loneliness has this layer that doesn't come up much: the gap isn't just between you and others. It's between you and yourself. Which means even if you solved every external problem — found the perfect people, created the perfect conditions — you'd still be lonely in the places where you haven't met yourself yet.

I'm not sure what to do with that. It's not a comforting realization.

## What seems to help

Not a solution. There might not be one. But some things seem to matter:

Time that isn't scheduled or bounded. Just being in the same space long enough for the scripts to run out and something unplanned to start. Mutual curiosity — not advice-giving, not problem-solving, just genuine interest in what's going on inside someone else. A tolerance for silence, the willingness to sit with someone without filling the space. A lot of depth happens after the easy things have been said and you're left with whatever's underneath.

No audience. Connection happens between two people. Not in groups, not on platforms, not performed for anyone else. And repeated contact — once isn't enough. You see someone differently the fifth time, the twentieth time. Depth accumulates.

Games [figured this out](/prose/what-do-we-keep-losing). You just join someone's world and exist in it together. The doing creates the connection. The rest of life made it so complicated that we forgot it used to be that simple. Or maybe it was never simple and we just remember it that way. Hard to tell.

## So what now

Some of this might just be the human condition. One consciousness trying to be understood by another. The permanent gap between your inner experience and anyone else's ability to perceive it.

That doesn't make the loneliness less real. But it changes what you're looking for. Maybe the goal isn't "never lonely." Maybe it's "sometimes truly known." And even that — you're not always sure you want it. Sometimes you think you want to be known and then someone actually sees you and it's terrifying and you retreat right back into the role. Sometimes the loneliness feels safer than the alternative and you hate that about yourself.

You can seek out the contexts that allow depth. You can be the one who breaks the script. You can find the people who want this too — and they're out there, feeling the same thing, waiting for someone to go first.

Whether you actually will is a different question.
